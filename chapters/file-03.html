Sonora, California is a city located in Tuolumne County. It is situated in the heart of California's Gold Country, in the Sierra Nevada foothills. Sonora is the county seat of Tuolumne County.

The following is based on Dellschau's unverified accounts. And newspaper articles.

The settlement of Sonora, about 130 miles east of San Francisco, was booming in the mid to late 1800s. In a saloon of one of the local boarding houses, a group of men got together every Friday night and talked of dreams of human flight.

They called themselves the Sonora Aero Club. Dellschau's writings describe the club as a secret group of flight enthusiasts. Over time, they counted more than 60 members. The Aero Club's rules were roughly once a quarter; each member had to stand before the group and tell how he would build an airship.

The club was not well known outside the local area. Later a set of 12 notebooks were found. These books are all the documentation that has been discovered about this group to date. The club is reported to have disbanded in 1860.

Charles A.A. Dellschau

Charles Dellschau was born in Berlin in 1830, Dellschau immigrated to the United States at age 19 and worked as a butcher near Houston Texas. He married a widow, raised three children, and served in the Confederate army during the American Civil War. After his wife and son died in 1877, Dellschau worked as a clerk for his son-in-law's saddlery until his death in 1923.

Some of the notebooks that were written and illustrated by Charles A.A. Dellschau.

In the 1960s, a house in Houston caught on fire. In the aftermath, a set of 12 scrapbooks were discovered. They depicted a society, the Sonora Aero Club, that had all but disappeared from history forty years later, a junk dealer uncovered the 12 notebooks, bound with shoelaces, from a trash heap outside the Dellschau family home after the fire. The notebooks changed hands a few times, eventually winding up in the care of art history student Mary Jane Victor.

The 12 notebooks were created by Charles Dellschau between 1908 and 1921 and document the invention of an anti-gravity fuel called NB Gas by a secret society of aviators called the Sonora Aero Club. Containing more than 2,500 drawings of flying machines, the diaries piece together newspaper clippings, diagrams, and coded language. The notebooks recount Dellschau's time living in a boarding house from 1854 to 1859. His neighbors included members of the Sonora Aero Club.

The club appointed Dellschau as their scribe, and he narrates the group's activities in extraordinary detail, describing many members by name. Peter Mennis, whose Aero Goosey was among the group's most successful crafts, Tosh Wilson, Louis Caro, and Jacob Mischer, whose plan to sell the designs for his airship ended in its sabotage and fiery crash.

Main Components in the "Aero Goosey" Drawing

Central Compass/Mandala: The entire design is structured around a central circular motif, which in Jungian analysis is interpreted as a mandala form, representing balance and centering. Structurally, it functions as the main body or frame of the airship.

- Central Hub/Motor: The very center of the circle is where the primary power source is located. According to the notebooks' cryptic descriptions, a "secret powder was added to water" and dripped onto a "special drum", which caused a chemical reaction to make the drum spin (clockwise?). This spinning action powered the entire apparatus, likely to include the components indicated.

- Side Drums/Compressor Motors: These are the large circular sections I highlighted with red arrows. They are described as housing the "compressor motors". These mechanisms were driven by the central engine and utilized the anti-gravity "NB Gas" for lift and propulsion.

- Paddles/wheels: These are shown opposite the Side Drums. The paddles would act as primiƟve propellers. These would provide very minimal thrust.

- "Parashoots": The text on the left mentions the "Aero Goosey" could be sheltered "by making sort of Parashoots". These refer to the large, parachute-like outer envelopes of the airship, which were likely designed to be filled or deflated to manage lift and aid in landing.

- "Press Blooms": Look closely at the material used for the drawing. Dellschau used newspaper clippings (which he called "press blooms") collaged directly into the sphere and margins of his drawings, often related to actual early flight attempts or disasters from the time. This blends real-world aeronautical news with his fantastical vision.

Weight and Balance: In actual aviation, the center of gravity is critical for stability. By offsetting the driving mechanism, the design might be attempting to use an asymmetrical force to generate lift or torque, rather than simple forward thrust. The offset position suggests a method of control or stability that relies on a specific distribution of weight and generated force.

The off-center nature is central to the mystery of both the historical sightings and Dellschau's art, as it suggests a mechanical operation entirely different from conventional aerodynamics.

Theories: Researchers who have studied the notebooks speculate that the gas was likely a real-world chemical compound that could be produced in simple 19th-century

Gallium can also be extracted from coal fly ash using a combination of chemical processes, including leaching with acids, followed by solvent extraction and precipitation steps.
